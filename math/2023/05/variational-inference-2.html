Variational Inference (2)

Modern Bayesian statistics relies on models for which the posterior is not easy to compute and corresponding algorithms for approximating them. Variational inference is one of the most popular methods for approximating the posterior. In this post, we will introduce the basic idea of variational inference and its application to a simple example.

Big picture again

Let’s review Bayesian formula first:

p(θ∣x)=p(x∣θ)p(θ)p(x)=p(x∣θ)p(θ)∫p(x∣θ)p(θ)dθposterior=likelihood×priormarginal likelihood∝likelihood×prior(1) \begin{aligned} p(\theta | x) &= \frac{p(x | \theta) p(\theta)}{p(x)} \\ &= \frac{p(x | \theta) p(\theta)}{\int p(x | \theta) p(\theta) d\theta} \\ \text{posterior} &= \frac{\text{likelihood} \times \text{prior}}{\text{marginal likelihood}} \\ & \propto \text{likelihood} \times \text{prior} \end{aligned} \tag{1} p(θ∣x)posterior​=p(x)p(x∣θ)p(θ)​=∫p(x∣θ)p(θ)dθp(x∣θ)p(θ)​=marginal likelihoodlikelihood×prior​∝likelihood×prior​(1)

where p(θ∣x)p(\theta | x)p(θ∣x) is the posterior distribution, p(x∣θ)p(x | \theta)p(x∣θ) is the likelihood, p(θ)p(\theta)p(θ) is the prior distribution, and p(x)p(x)p(x) is the marginal likelihood.

Usually, we will set up a prior distribution first. With the prior distribution, we can have the value of p(θ)p(\theta)p(θ), and then we can calculate the posterior distribution p(θ∣x)p(\theta | x)p(θ∣x). However, when we update the posterior distribution, we need to calculate the marginal likelihood p(x)p(x)p(x), which is the denominator of the formula.

For the continuous case, the marginal likelihood is:

p(x)=∫p(x∣θ)p(θ)dθ p(x) = \int p(x | \theta) p(\theta) d\theta p(x)=∫p(x∣θ)p(θ)dθ

For the discrete case, the marginal likelihood is:

p(x)=∑θp(x∣θ)p(θ) p(x) = \sum_{\theta} p(x | \theta) p(\theta) p(x)=θ∑​p(x∣θ)p(θ)

However, when we have many hidden variables, the marginal likelihood is hard to calculate and the exact calculation sometimes is prohibitively expensive. To solve this problem, we need to resort to approximation schemes, and these fall broadly into two categories, according to whether they rely on stochastic or deterministic approximations: Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) and variational inference (VI).

Since sampling methods can be computationally expensive, especially for the large data sets that are increasingly common in modern applications. In contrast, variational inference can scale well to large data sets and high-dimensional models.

Bayesian mixture of Gaussians

Based on the paper by Blei et al. (Blei et al., 2017), we will use a Bayesian mixture of Gaussians to illustrate the basic idea of variational inference. The mixture of Gaussians is a simple model that can be used for clustering and density estimation.

Consider a Bayesian mixture of unit-variance univariate Gaussians (we use univariate Gaussians for simplicity, but the model can be easily extended to multivariate Gaussians). The model has KKK components, each with a mean μk\mu_kμk​, such as μ={μ1,μ2,...,μK}\mu = \{\mu_1, \mu_2, ..., \mu_K\}μ={μ1​,μ2​,...,μK​}. The mean parameter is drawn independently from a common prior p(μk)p(\mu_k)p(μk​), which we assume to be a Gaussian N(0,σ2)N(0, \sigma^2)N(0,σ2), the prior variance σ2\sigma^2σ2 is a hyper-parameter.

Here is the data generating process:

  • For each data point xix_ixi​ in NNN data points:
    • Sample a cluster assignment ziz_izi​ uniformly: zi∼Uniform(K)z_i \sim \text{Uniform}(K)zi​∼Uniform(K)
    • Sample a data point xix_ixi​ from a Gaussian with mean μzi\mu_{z_i}μzi​​: xi∼N(μzi,1)x_i \sim N(\mu_{z_i}, 1)xi​∼N(μzi​​,1)
      • where μzi\mu_{z_i}μzi​​ is drawn from a Gaussian with mean 000 and variance σ2\sigma^2σ2: μzi∼N(0,σ2)\mu_{z_i} \sim N(0, \sigma^2)μzi​​∼N(0,σ2) with p(μzi)p(\mu_{z_i})p(μzi​​) being the prior distribution of μzi\mu_{z_i}μzi​​.

We can write down the joint distribution of the model:

p(x,μ,z)=p(x∣μ,z)p(μ)p(z)=∏i=1Np(xi∣μzi,zi)p(μzi)p(zi)(2) \begin{aligned} p(x, \mu, z) & = p(x | \mu, z) p(\mu) p(z) \\ & = \prod_{i=1}^N p(x_i | \mu_{z_i}, z_i) p(\mu_{z_i}) p(z_i) \\ \end{aligned} \tag{2} p(x,μ,z)​=p(x∣μ,z)p(μ)p(z)=i=1∏N​p(xi​∣μzi​​,zi​)p(μzi​​)p(zi​)​(2)

Now, if we encode ziz_izi​ as a one-hot vector zi=[0,0,...,1,...,0]z_i = [0, 0, ..., 1, ..., 0]zi​=[0,0,...,1,...,0], where the 111 is in the kkkth position, and the generative process can be represented by the following full hierarchical model:

μk∼N(0,σ2),k=1,...,Kzi∼Categorical(1/K,⋯ ,1/K),i=1,...,Nxi∣zi,μ∼N(ziTμ,1),i=1,...,N(3) \begin{aligned} \mu_k & \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2), \quad & k = 1, ..., K \\ z_i & \sim \text{Categorical}(1/K, \cdots, 1/K), \quad & i = 1, ..., N \\ x_i | z_i, \mu & \sim \mathcal{N}(z_i^T \mu, 1), \quad & i = 1, ..., N \end{aligned} \tag{3} μk​zi​xi​∣zi​,μ​∼N(0,σ2),∼Categorical(1/K,⋯,1/K),∼N(ziT​μ,1),​k=1,...,Ki=1,...,Ni=1,...,N​(3)

Then the joint distribution of the model can be written as:

p(x,μ,z)=p(x∣μ,z)p(μ)p(z)=∏i=1Np(xi∣μ,zi)p(μ)p(zi) \begin{aligned} p(x, \mu, z) & = p(x| \mu, z) p(\mu) p(z) \\ & = \prod_{i=1}^N p(x_i | \mu, z_i) p(\mu) p(z_i) \\ \end{aligned} p(x,μ,z)​=p(x∣μ,z)p(μ)p(z)=i=1∏N​p(xi​∣μ,zi​)p(μ)p(zi​)​

Remark: The one-hot vector ziz_izi​ is a vector of length KKK with all zeros except for a single one in the kkkth position. The kkkth position indicates the cluster assignment of the iiith data point xix_ixi​. The mean vector μ\muμ is a vector of length KKK with each element μk\mu_kμk​ being the mean of the kkkth cluster.

The latent variables are {μ,z}\{\mu, z\}{μ,z}, which are KKK means and NNN cluster assignments. The observed variables are {x}\{x\}{x}, which are NNN data points.

The evidence (or marginal likelihood) is:

p(x)=∫μp(μ)∑zip(xi∣μ,zi)p(zi)dμ=∑ziKp(zi)∫μp(μ)p(xi∣μ,zi)dμ(4) \begin{aligned} p(x) & = \int_{\mu} p(\mu) \sum_{z_i} p(x_i | \mu, z_i) p(z_i) d\mu \\ & = \sum_{z_i}^K p (z_i) \int_{\mu} p(\mu) p(x_i | \mu, z_i) d\mu \\ \end{aligned} \tag{4} p(x)​=∫μ​p(μ)zi​∑​p(xi​∣μ,zi​)p(zi​)dμ=zi​∑K​p(zi​)∫μ​p(μ)p(xi​∣μ,zi​)dμ​(4)

Although it is possible to calculate the marginal likelihood p(x)p(x)p(x), it is computationally expensive:

  • calculate the Gaussian integral ∫μp(μ)p(xi∣μ,zi)dμ\int_{\mu} p(\mu) p(x_i | \mu, z_i) d\mu∫μ​p(μ)p(xi​∣μ,zi​)dμ for each data point xix_ixi​ by calculating a Gaussian prior p(μ)p(\mu)p(μ) and a Gaussian likelihood p(xi∣μ,zi)p(x_i | \mu, z_i)p(xi​∣μ,zi​)
  • sum over all possible cluster assignments ziz_izi​.

This means that we need to calculate KNK^NKN Gaussian integrals (or the complexity is O(KN)\mathcal{O}(K^N)O(KN)) , which is prohibitively expensive for large KKK and NNN.

This example illustrates what we mean by the marginal likelihood being intractable.

To solve this problem, we will use variational inference to approximate the marginal likelihood or evidence p(x)p(x)p(x) by searching for a distribution q(μ,z)q(\mu, z)q(μ,z) that is close to the true posterior p(μ,z∣x)p(\mu, z | x)p(μ,z∣x).

Evidence lower bound (ELBO)

For a general Beyesian model, suppose we have latent variable zzz and observed variable xxx, the joint distribution of the model is:

p(z∣x)=p(x,z)p(x)=p(x,z)∫zp(x,z)dz(5) p(z | x) = \frac{p(x, z)}{p(x)} = \frac{p(x, z)}{\int_z p(x, z) dz} \tag{5} p(z∣x)=p(x)p(x,z)​=∫z​p(x,z)dzp(x,z)​(5)

Equation (5) gives:

p(x)=p(x,z)p(z∣x)(6) p(x) = \frac{p(x, z)}{p(z | x)} \tag{6} p(x)=p(z∣x)p(x,z)​(6)

Now, for the evidence p(x)p(x)p(x), we can write it as:

ln⁡p(x)=∫zq(z)dzln⁡p(x)∫zq(z)dz=1=∫zq(z)dzln⁡p(x,z)p(z∣x)=∫zq(z)ln⁡p(x,z)p(z∣x)dz=∫zq(z)ln⁡(p(x,z)q(z)q(z)p(z∣x))dz=∫zq(z)ln⁡(p(x,z)q(z))dz+∫zq(z)ln⁡(q(z)p(z∣x))dz=L(x)+KL(q∣∣p)(6) \begin{aligned} \ln p(x) & = \int_z q(z) dz \ln p(x) \quad \text{$\int_z q(z) dz = 1$} \\ & = \int_z q(z) dz \ln \frac{p(x, z)}{p(z | x)} \\ & = \int_z q(z)\ln \frac{p(x, z)}{p(z | x)} dz \\ & = \int_z q(z) \ln \left ( \frac{p(x, z)}{q(z)} \frac{q(z)}{p(z | x)} \right ) dz \\ & = \int_z q(z) \ln \left ( \frac{p(x, z)}{q(z)} \right ) dz + \int_z q(z) \ln \left ( \frac{q(z)}{p(z | x)} \right ) dz \\ & = L(x) + KL(q || p) \end{aligned} \tag{6} lnp(x)​=∫z​q(z)dzlnp(x)∫z​q(z)dz=1=∫z​q(z)dzlnp(z∣x)p(x,z)​=∫z​q(z)lnp(z∣x)p(x,z)​dz=∫z​q(z)ln(q(z)p(x,z)​p(z∣x)q(z)​)dz=∫z​q(z)ln(q(z)p(x,z)​)dz+∫z​q(z)ln(p(z∣x)q(z)​)dz=L(x)+KL(q∣∣p)​(6)

where L(x)L(x)L(x) is the evidence lower bound (ELBO) and KL(q∣∣p)KL(q || p)KL(q∣∣p) is the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the variational distribution q(z)q(z)q(z) and the true posterior p(z∣x)p(z | x)p(z∣x), which is non-negative and bounded by zero.

As before, we can maximize the ELBO L(x)L(x)L(x) to maximize the evidence p(x)p(x)p(x) by optimization with respect to the variational distribution q(z)q(z)q(z), which is equivalent to minimizing the KL divergence KL(q∣∣p)KL(q || p)KL(q∣∣p). Since working with the true posterior p(z∣x)p(z | x)p(z∣x) is intractable, we therefore consider a family of variational distributions q(z)q(z)q(z) that are easy to work with, which will make the optimization of the ELBO L(x)L(x)L(x) tractable.

Now, for the ELBO L(x)L(x)L(x), we have:

L(x)=∫zq(z)ln⁡(p(x,z)q(z))dz=∫zq(z)ln⁡(p(x∣z)p(z)q(z))dz=∫zq(z)[ln⁡p(x∣z)+ln⁡p(z)q(z)]dz=∫zq(z)ln⁡p(x∣z)dz−∫zq(z)ln⁡q(z)p(z)dz=Eq(z)[ln⁡p(x∣z)]−KL(q(z)∣∣p(z))=Eq(z)[ln⁡p(x,z)p(z)]−KL(q(z)∣∣p(z))=Eq(z)[ln⁡p(x,z)]−Eq(z)[ln⁡p(z)]−KL(q(z)∣∣p(z))=Eq(z)[ln⁡p(x,z)]−H(q(z))−KL(q(z)∣∣p(z))(7) \begin{aligned} L(x) & = \int_z q(z) \ln \left ( \frac{p(x, z)}{q(z)} \right ) dz \\ & = \int_z q(z) \ln \left ( \frac{p(x|z)p(z)}{q(z)} \right ) dz \\ & = \int_z q(z) \left [ \ln p(x|z) + \ln \frac{p(z)}{q(z)} \right] dz \\ & = \int_z q(z) \ln p(x|z) dz - \int_z q(z) \ln \frac{q(z)}{p(z)} dz \\ & = \mathbb{E}_{q(z)} \left [ \ln p(x|z) \right ] - KL(q(z) || p(z)) \\ & = \mathbb{E}_{q(z)} \left [ \ln \frac{p(x, z)}{p(z)} \right ] - KL(q(z) || p(z)) \\ & = \mathbb{E}_{q(z)} \left [ \ln p(x, z) \right ] - \mathbb{E}_{q(z)} \left [ \ln p(z) \right ] - KL(q(z) || p(z)) \\ & = \mathbb{E}_{q(z)} \left [ \ln p(x, z) \right ] - \mathcal{H}(q(z)) - KL(q(z) || p(z)) \end{aligned} \tag{7} L(x)​=∫z​q(z)ln(q(z)p(x,z)​)dz=∫z​q(z)ln(q(z)p(x∣z)p(z)​)dz=∫z​q(z)[lnp(x∣z)+lnq(z)p(z)​]dz=∫z​q(z)lnp(x∣z)dz−∫z​q(z)lnp(z)q(z)​dz=Eq(z)​[lnp(x∣z)]−KL(q(z)∣∣p(z))=Eq(z)​[lnp(z)p(x,z)​]−KL(q(z)∣∣p(z))=Eq(z)​[lnp(x,z)]−Eq(z)​[lnp(z)]−KL(q(z)∣∣p(z))=Eq(z)​[lnp(x,z)]−H(q(z))−KL(q(z)∣∣p(z))​(7)

The ELBO L(x)L(x)L(x) is the sum of three terms:

  • the first term Eq(z)[ln⁡p(x,z)]\mathbb{E}_{q(z)} \left [ \ln p(x, z) \right ]Eq(z)​[lnp(x,z)] is the expected log joint probability of the observed and latent variables under the variational distribution q(z)q(z)q(z)
  • the second term H(q(z))\mathcal{H}(q(z))H(q(z)) is the entropy of the variational distribution q(z)q(z)q(z)
  • the third term KL(q(z)∣∣p(z))KL(q(z) || p(z))KL(q(z)∣∣p(z)) is the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the variational distribution q(z)q(z)q(z) and the prior p(z)p(z)p(z).

The intuition behind the ELBO L(x)L(x)L(x) is that we can condition on the latent variable zzz and maximize the expected log joint probability Eq(z)[ln⁡p(x,z)]\mathbb{E}_{q(z)} \left [ \ln p(x, z) \right ]Eq(z)​[lnp(x,z)] to maximize the ELBO L(x)L(x)L(x), which is equivalent to maximizing the evidence p(x)p(x)p(x). This means we do not have to consider the intractable marginal likelihood p(x)p(x)p(x) directly.

How could we choose the variational distribution q(z)q(z)q(z)? We can choose the variational distribution q(z)q(z)q(z) to be a member of the exponential family, which is a family of distributions that are easy to work with. The coming section will show how to choose the variational distribution q(z)q(z)q(z).

Mean field theory

Mean field theory is a variational inference method that assumes that the latent variables zzz are independent of each other. This means that we can factorize the variational distribution q(z)q(z)q(z) as:

q(z)=∏i=1Nqi(zi)(8) q(z) = \prod_{i=1}^N q_i(z_i) \tag{8} q(z)=i=1∏N​qi​(zi​)(8)

where qi(zi)q_i(z_i)qi​(zi​) is the variational distribution for the iii-th latent variable ziz_izi​.

Coordinate ascent mean-field variational inference

By factorizing the variational distribution q(z)q(z)q(z), we can maximize the ELBO L(x)L(x)L(x) by coordinate ascent. This means that we can maximize the ELBO L(x)L(x)L(x) with respect to each variational distribution qi(zi)q_i(z_i)qi​(zi​) while holding the other variational distributions qj(zj)q_j(z_j)qj​(zj​) for j≠ij \neq ij​=i fixed. This is equivalent to minimizing the KL divergence KL(q∣∣p)KL(q || p)KL(q∣∣p) with respect to each variational distribution qi(zi)q_i(z_i)qi​(zi​) while holding the other variational distributions qj(zj)q_j(z_j)qj​(zj​) for j≠ij \neq ij​=i fixed.

Here is the algorithm for coordinate ascent mean-field variational inference:

  1. choose a family of variational distributions q(z)q(z)q(z)
  2. compute the ELBO L(x)L(x)L(x)
  3. maximize the ELBO L(x)L(x)L(x) with respect to each variational distribution qi(zi)q_i(z_i)qi​(zi​) while holding the other variational distributions qj(zj)q_j(z_j)qj​(zj​) for j≠ij \neq ij​=i fixed
  4. repeat step 2 and 3 until convergence

The reason we could do this is that the factorization of the variational distribution q(z)q(z)q(z) allows us to optimize each variational distribution qi(zi)q_i(z_i)qi​(zi​) independently and the computation becomes tractable with log-likelihoods and expectations (the production in equation (8) becomes a summation).

The first term in the ELBO L(x)L(x)L(x)

First, let’s write down the joint probability of the observed and latent variables, which is the first term in the ELBO L(x)L(x)L(x) (equation (10)):

ln⁡p(x,z,μ)=ln⁡p(μ)+ln⁡p(z)+ln⁡p(x∣z,μ)=∑jln⁡p(μj)+∑i[ln⁡p(zi)+ln⁡p(xi∣zi,μ)]=∑jln⁡p(μj)+∑iln⁡p(xi∣zi,μ)+const(13) \begin{aligned} \ln p(x, z, \mu) & = \ln p(\mu) + \ln p(z) + \ln p(x|z, \mu) \\ & = \sum_j \ln p(\mu_j) + \sum_i [ \ln p(z_i) + \ln p(x_i|z_i, \mu) ]\\ & = \sum_j \ln p(\mu_j) + \sum_i \ln p(x_i|z_i, \mu) + \text{const} \end{aligned} \tag{13} lnp(x,z,μ)​=lnp(μ)+lnp(z)+lnp(x∣z,μ)=j∑​lnp(μj​)+i∑​[lnp(zi​)+lnp(xi​∣zi​,μ)]=j∑​lnp(μj​)+i∑​lnp(xi​∣zi​,μ)+const​(13)

Entropy of the variational distribution

Now, we will compute the entropy of the variational distribution q(μ,z)q(\mu, z)q(μ,z), which is the second term in the ELBO L(x)L(x)L(x) (equation (10)):

ln⁡q(μ,z)=ln⁡q(μ)+ln⁡q(z)=∑iln⁡q(zi)+∑jln⁡q(μj)=∑iln⁡Categorical(ϕi)+∑jln⁡N(mj,sj2)based on equation (11)=∑iln⁡[∏kϕikzik]+∑jln⁡[12πsj2exp⁡(−(μj−mj)22sj2)]=∑i∑kzikln⁡ϕik+∑j[−12ln⁡(2πsj2)−(μj−mj)22sj2](17) \begin{aligned} \ln q(\mu, z) & = \ln q(\mu) + \ln q(z) \\ & = \sum_i \ln q(z_i) + \sum_j \ln q(\mu_j) \\ & = \sum_i \ln \text{Categorical}(\phi_i) + \sum_j \ln \mathcal{N}(m_j, s_j^2) \quad \text{based on equation (11)} \\ & = \sum_i \ln \left [ \prod_k \phi_{ik}^{z_{ik}} \right ] + \sum_j \ln \left [ \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi s_j^2}} \exp \left ( - \frac{(\mu_j - m_j)^2}{2s_j^2} \right ) \right ] \\ & = \sum_i \sum_k z_{ik} \ln \phi_{ik} + \sum_j \left [ -\frac{1}{2} \ln (2\pi s_j^2) - \frac{(\mu_j - m_j)^2}{2s_j^2} \right ] \\ \end{aligned} \tag{17} lnq(μ,z)​=lnq(μ)+lnq(z)=i∑​lnq(zi​)+j∑​lnq(μj​)=i∑​lnCategorical(ϕi​)+j∑​lnN(mj​,sj2​)based on equation (11)=i∑​ln[k∏​ϕikzik​​]+j∑​ln⎣⎢⎡​2πsj2​​1​exp(−2sj2​(μj​−mj​)2​)⎦⎥⎤​=i∑​k∑​zik​lnϕik​+j∑​[−21​ln(2πsj2​)−2sj2​(μj​−mj​)2​]​(17)

Therefore, the ElBO L(x)L(x)L(x) is:

L(x)∝∑jEq[−μj22σ2]−∑i∑kEq[zik(xi−μk)22]+∑i∑kzikEq[ln⁡ϕik]+∑jEq[−12ln⁡(2πsj2)−(μj−mj)22sj2](18) \begin{aligned} L(x) \propto & \sum_j \mathbb{E_q} \left [ - \frac{\mu_j^2}{2\sigma^2} \right ] - \sum_i \sum_k \mathbb{E_q} \left [ z_{ik} \frac{(x_i - \mu_k)^2}{2} \right ] + \\ & \sum_i \sum_k z_{ik} \mathbb{E_q} [ \ln \phi_{ik}] + \sum_j \mathbb{E_q} \left [ -\frac{1}{2} \ln (2\pi s_j^2) - \frac{(\mu_j - m_j)^2}{2s_j^2} \right ] \end{aligned} \tag{18} L(x)∝​j∑​Eq​[−2σ2μj2​​]−i∑​k∑​Eq​[zik​2(xi​−μk​)2​]+i∑​k∑​zik​Eq​[lnϕik​]+j∑​Eq​[−21​ln(2πsj2​)−2sj2​(μj​−mj​)2​]​(18)

With equation (18) we can maximize the ELBO L(x)L(x)L(x) with respect to the variational parameters ϕ\phiϕ and m,s2m, s^2m,s2 by taking the derivatives of L(x)L(x)L(x) with respect to ϕ\phiϕ and m,s2m, s^2m,s2 and set them to zero.

We will not go through the derivation of the update equations for ϕ\phiϕ and m,s2m, s^2m,s2 here. Instead, we will just write down the update equations:

ϕik∗∝exp⁡[−12(mj2+sj2)+ximj]mj∗=∑iϕijxi1σ2+∑iϕijsj2=11σ2+∑iϕij(19) \begin{aligned} \phi_{ik}^* & \propto \exp \left [ - \frac{1}{2} (m_j^2 + s_j^2) + x_im_j \right ] \\ m_j^* & = \frac{\sum_i \phi_{ij}x_i}{\frac{1}{\sigma^2} + \sum_{i} \phi_{ij}} \\ s_j^2 & = \frac{1}{\frac{1}{\sigma^2} + \sum_{i} \phi_{ij}} \end{aligned} \tag{19} ϕik∗​mj∗​sj2​​∝exp[−21​(mj2​+sj2​)+xi​mj​]=σ21​+∑i​ϕij​∑i​ϕij​xi​​=σ21​+∑i​ϕij​1​​(19)

class UGMM:
    """
    Univariate Gaussian Mixture Model
    """
    
    def __init__(self, X, K = 2, sigma = 1):
        self.X = X
        self.K = K
        self.N = X.shape[0]
        self.sigma2 = sigma**2
        
        # initialize the parameters
        # using dirichlet distribution to initialize the prior probability
        # we fix alpha in the range of [1, 10] for initialization
        # it can be changed to other values
        alpha_const = np.random.random()*np.random.randint(1, 10)
        self.phi = np.random.dirichlet([alpha_const]*self.K, size=self.N)
        # initialize the mean from uniform distribution
        self.m = np.random.uniform(min(self.X), max(self.X), self.K)
        # initialize the variance from uniform distribution
        self.s2 = np.random.uniform(0, 1, self.K)
        
    def _get_elbo(self):
        # calculate the evidence lower bound
        # term 1 in euqation (14)
        # although we use sigma^2 in equation (14) but we use s2 in the code
        # because we are not estimating sigma^2 but s2 (variational inference)
        elbo_term1 = np.log(self.s2) - self.m / self.s2
        elbo_term1 = elbo_term1.sum()
        # term is not exactly the same as equation (14)
        # herer we penalize the model with large variance
        # term 2 based on equation (17)
        # again the term is not exactly the same as equation (17)
        # but proportional to it
        elbo_term2 = -0.5 * np.add.outer(self.X**2, self.s2+self.m**2)
        elbo_term2 += np.outer(self.X, self.m)
        elbo_term2 -= np.log(self.phi)
        elbo_term2 *= self.phi
        elbo_term2 = elbo_term2.sum()
        
        return elbo_term1 + elbo_term2
    
    def _update_phi(self):
        t1 = np.outer(self.X, self.m)
        t2 = -(0.5*self.m**2 + 0.5*self.s2)
        exponent = t1 + t2[np.newaxis, :]
        self.phi = np.exp(exponent)
        self.phi = self.phi / self.phi.sum(1)[:, np.newaxis]
        
    def _update_m(self):
        self.m = (self.phi*self.X[:, np.newaxis]).sum(0) * (1/self.sigma2 + self.phi.sum(0))**(-1)
        assert self.m.size == self.K
        #print(self.m)
        self.s2 = (1/self.sigma2 + self.phi.sum(0))**(-1)
        assert self.s2.size == self.K
        
    def _cavi(self):
        self._update_phi()
        self._update_m()
    
    def fit(self, max_iter=100, tol=1e-10):
        # fit the model
        self.elbos = [self._get_elbo()]
        self.track_m = [self.m.copy()]
        self.track_s2 = [self.s2.copy()]
        
        for iter_ in range(1, max_iter+1):
            self._cavi()
            self.track_m.append(self.m.copy())
            self.track_s2.append(self.s2.copy())
            self.elbos.append(self._get_elbo())
            
            if iter_ % 10 == 0:
                print("Iteration: {}, ELBO: {}".format(iter_, self.elbos[-1]))
                
            if np.abs(self.elbos[-1] - self.elbos[-2]) < tol:
                # print convergence information at iteration i
                print("Converged at iteration: {}, ELBO: {}".format(iter_,
                                                                        self.elbos[-1]))
                break

Summary and reflection

In this post, we derived the CAVI algorithm for Gaussian mixture model. We also implemented the algorithm in Python. However, one can see that it is not easy to derive the closed-form update for the parameters. It is also not very intuitive to implement the algorithm even in Python. That’s why people invented probabilistic programming languages such as Stan, PyMC3, Edward, Pyro, numpyro, etc.

Please read this post for more information about probabilistic programming languages.

Some people are trying to introduce variational inference in undergraduate statistics courses: this paper and this repository.

Right now, variational inference is still a very active research area. There are many new algorithms and applications, especially for large datasets. I hope this post can help you understand the basic idea of variational inference.

If you want to study more about probabilistic programming languages, I think numpyro is a good choice. It is based on JAX and Pyro. It is very easy to use and has a very active community. Following a right community is very important for learning new concepts in machine learning and deep learning.
