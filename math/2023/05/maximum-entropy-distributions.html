Maximum Entropy Distributions

The connection between entropy and probability distributions is really interesting. In this post, I will explore the connection between entropy and probability distributions, and how we can use this connection to derive the most likely probability distribution given some constraints.

When I was studying mathematics in university, one of my professors asked me why I switched from economics to mathematics. I told him that I was hooked by the normal distribution. To me, the phenomenon that the normal distribution appears everywhere in nature is really fascinating. I want to understand why it is so common. There might be some deep reason behind it. Because I want to find the secret of nature, I fall in love with mathematics.

To understand why the normal distribution is so common, we could look at it from the perspective of convolution. The normal distribution is the convolution of many independent random variables. The central limit theorem tells us that the convolution of many independent random variables will converge to the normal distribution. This is why the normal distribution is so common.

However, there is another way to look at it. The normal distribution is the probability distribution that has the maximum entropy. In this post, I will explore the connection between entropy and probability distributions, and how we can use this connection to derive the most likely probability distribution given some constraints.

Entropy

Entropy is a measure of uncertainty. The higher the entropy, the more uncertain we are. The entropy of a discrete random variable XXX is defined as

H(X)=−∑x∈Xp(x)log⁡p(x)(1) H(X) = - \sum_{x \in \mathcal{X}} p(x) \log p(x) \tag{1} H(X)=−x∈X∑​p(x)logp(x)(1)

where X\mathcal{X}X is the set of all possible values of XXX. The entropy of a continuous random variable XXX is defined as

H(X)=−∫x∈Xp(x)log⁡p(x)(2) H(X) = - \int_{x \in \mathcal{X}} p(x) \log p(x) \tag{2} H(X)=−∫x∈X​p(x)logp(x)(2)

where X\mathcal{X}X is the set of all possible values of XXX.

Maximum entropy distribution

Suppose we have a random variable XXX with a probability distribution p(x)p(x)p(x). We want to find the probability distribution p(x)p(x)p(x) that has the maximum entropy. In other words, we want to find the probability distribution p(x)p(x)p(x) that maximizes the entropy H(X)H(X)H(X). We will use natural logarithm in this post from now on.

max⁡p(x)H(X)=−∫abp(x)ln⁡p(x)dx(3) \max_{p(x)} H(X) = - \int_a^b p(x) \ln p(x) dx \tag{3} p(x)max​H(X)=−∫ab​p(x)lnp(x)dx(3)

We can solve this problem using the method of Lagrange multipliers. We introduce a Lagrange multiplier λ\lambdaλ and solve the following problem:

max⁡p(x)H(X)−λ(∫x∈Xp(x)−1)(4) \max_{p(x)} H(X) - \lambda \left( \int_{x \in \mathcal{X}} p(x) - 1 \right) \tag{4} p(x)max​H(X)−λ(∫x∈X​p(x)−1)(4)

We can solve this problem by taking the derivative of the objective function with respect to p(x)p(x)p(x) and set it to zero:

∂∂p(x)[−∫abp(x)ln⁡p(x)dx+λ(∫x∈Xp(x)−1)]=0(5) \frac{\partial}{\partial p(x)} \left[ - \int_a^b p(x) \ln p(x) dx + \lambda \left( \int_{x \in \mathcal{X}} p(x) - 1 \right) \right] = 0 \tag{5} ∂p(x)∂​[−∫ab​p(x)lnp(x)dx+λ(∫x∈X​p(x)−1)]=0(5)

We can solve this equation and get

−1−ln⁡p(x)+λ=0⇒p(x)=eλ−1(6) -1 - \ln p(x) + \lambda = 0 \Rightarrow p(x) = e^{ \lambda - 1} \tag{6} −1−lnp(x)+λ=0⇒p(x)=eλ−1(6)

We can use the constraint ∫x∈Xp(x)−1=0\int_{x \in \mathcal{X}} p(x) - 1 = 0∫x∈X​p(x)−1=0 to solve for λ\lambdaλ:

∫x∈Xp(x)−1=0⇒∫x∈Xeλ−1dx=1(7) \int_{x \in \mathcal{X}} p(x) - 1 = 0 \Rightarrow \int_{x \in \mathcal{X}} e^{\lambda -1} dx = 1 \tag{7} ∫x∈X​p(x)−1=0⇒∫x∈X​eλ−1dx=1(7)

We can solve this equation and get

∫x∈Xeλ−1dx=1⇒∫x∈Xeλ−1dx=eλ−1∫x∈Xdx=1⇒eλ−1=1∫x∈Xdx=1b−a⇒λ=ln⁡(1b−a)+1 \begin{aligned} \int_{x \in \mathcal{X}} e^{\lambda -1} dx = 1 &\Rightarrow \int_{x \in \mathcal{X}} e^{\lambda -1} dx = e^{\lambda -1} \int_{x \in \mathcal{X}} dx = 1 \\ &\Rightarrow e^{\lambda -1} = \frac{1}{\int_{x \in \mathcal{X}} dx} = \frac{1}{b - a} \\ &\Rightarrow \lambda = \ln (\frac{1}{b - a}) + 1 \end{aligned} ∫x∈X​eλ−1dx=1​⇒∫x∈X​eλ−1dx=eλ−1∫x∈X​dx=1⇒eλ−1=∫x∈X​dx1​=b−a1​⇒λ=ln(b−a1​)+1​

We can plug λ\lambdaλ back into equation (6) and get

p(x)=1b−a(8) p(x) = \frac{1}{b - a} \tag{8} p(x)=b−a1​(8)

This is the uniform distribution. The uniform distribution is the probability distribution that has the maximum entropy when we only have one constraint that p(x)p(x)p(x) is a probability distribution.

This should not be too unexpected. It is quite intuitive that a uniform distribution is the maximal ignorance distribution (when no other constraints were made). If we know nothing about the distribution of XXX, then the uniform distribution is the best guess we can make.

Maximum entropy distribution with mean and variance constraints

For a random variable XXX with a probability distribution p(x)p(x)p(x), we can define the mean and variance as follows:

E[X]=∫x∈Xxp(x)dx=μV[X]=∫x∈X(x−μ)2p(x)dx(9) \begin{aligned} \mathbb{E}[X] &= \int_{x \in \mathcal{X}} x p(x) dx = \mu \\ \mathbb{V}[X] &= \int_{x \in \mathcal{X}} (x - \mu)^2 p(x) dx \end{aligned} \tag{9} E[X]V[X]​=∫x∈X​xp(x)dx=μ=∫x∈X​(x−μ)2p(x)dx​(9)

Now we will solve the following problem:

max⁡p(x)H(X)+λ1(∫x∈Xp(x)−1)−+λ2(∫x∈Xxp(x)−μ)+λ3(∫x∈X(x−E[X])2p(x)−σ2)(10) \max_{p(x)} H(X) + \lambda_1 \left( \int_{x \in \mathcal{X}} p(x) - 1 \right) -+ \lambda_2 \left( \int_{x \in \mathcal{X}} x p(x) - \mu \right) + \lambda_3 \left( \int_{x \in \mathcal{X}} (x - \mathbb{E}[X])^2 p(x) - \sigma^2 \right) \tag{10} p(x)max​H(X)+λ1​(∫x∈X​p(x)−1)−+λ2​(∫x∈X​xp(x)−μ)+λ3​(∫x∈X​(x−E[X])2p(x)−σ2)(10)

Since μ\muμ is included in the variance, we only need to solve for λ1\lambda_1λ1​ and λ3\lambda_3λ3​. Therefore, we could solve the following problem:

L=max⁡p(x)H(X)+λ1(∫x∈Xp(x)dx−1)+λ3(∫x∈X(x−μ)2p(x)dx−σ2)(11) \mathcal{L} = \max_{p(x)} H(X) + \lambda_1 \left( \int_{x \in \mathcal{X}} p(x) dx - 1 \right) + \lambda_3 \left( \int_{x \in \mathcal{X}} (x - \mu)^2 p(x) dx - \sigma^2 \right) \tag{11} L=p(x)max​H(X)+λ1​(∫x∈X​p(x)dx−1)+λ3​(∫x∈X​(x−μ)2p(x)dx−σ2)(11)

Now, we will take the derivative of the objective function with respect to p(x)p(x)p(x) and set it to zero:

∂∂p(x)[−∫abp(x)ln⁡p(x)dx+λ1(∫x∈Xp(x)−1)+λ3(∫x∈X(x−μ)2p(x)−σ2)]=0(12) \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial}{\partial p(x)} \bigg [ - \int_a^b p(x) \ln p(x) dx & + \lambda_1 \left( \int_{x \in \mathcal{X}} p(x) - 1 \right) \\ & + \lambda_3 \left( \int_{x \in \mathcal{X}} (x - \mu)^2 p(x) - \sigma^2 \right) \bigg ] = 0 \end{aligned} \tag{12} ∂p(x)∂​[−∫ab​p(x)lnp(x)dx​+λ1​(∫x∈X​p(x)−1)+λ3​(∫x∈X​(x−μ)2p(x)−σ2)]=0​(12)

This gives us

−1−ln⁡p(x)+λ1+λ3(x−μ)2=0⇒p(x)=eλ1−1+λ3(x−μ)2(13) -1 - \ln p(x) + \lambda_1 + \lambda_3 (x - \mu)^2 = 0 \Rightarrow p(x) = e^{ \lambda_1 - 1 + \lambda_3 (x - \mu)^2} \tag{13} −1−lnp(x)+λ1​+λ3​(x−μ)2=0⇒p(x)=eλ1​−1+λ3​(x−μ)2(13)

Now, we will use the constraint ∫x∈Xp(x)−1=0\int_{x \in \mathcal{X}} p(x) - 1 = 0∫x∈X​p(x)−1=0 and variance constraint ∫x∈X(x−μ)2p(x)−σ2=0\int_{x \in \mathcal{X}} (x - \mu)^2 p(x) - \sigma^2 = 0∫x∈X​(x−μ)2p(x)−σ2=0 to solve for λ1\lambda_1λ1​ and λ3\lambda_3λ3​:

∫x∈Xp(x)−1=0⇒∫x∈Xeλ1−1+λ3(x−μ)2dx=1∫x∈X(x−μ)2p(x)−σ2=0⇒∫x∈X(x−μ)2eλ1−1+λ3(x−μ)2dx=σ2(14) \begin{aligned} \int_{x \in \mathcal{X}} p(x) - 1 = 0 &\Rightarrow \int_{x \in \mathcal{X}} e^{\lambda_1 - 1 + \lambda_3 (x - \mu)^2} dx = 1 \\ \int_{x \in \mathcal{X}} (x - \mu)^2 p(x) - \sigma^2 = 0 &\Rightarrow \int_{x \in \mathcal{X}} (x - \mu)^2 e^{\lambda_1 - 1 + \lambda_3 (x - \mu)^2} dx = \sigma^2 \end{aligned} \tag{14} ∫x∈X​p(x)−1=0∫x∈X​(x−μ)2p(x)−σ2=0​⇒∫x∈X​eλ1​−1+λ3​(x−μ)2dx=1⇒∫x∈X​(x−μ)2eλ1​−1+λ3​(x−μ)2dx=σ2​(14)

For the first constraint, we can solve it and get

∫x∈Xeλ1−1+λ3(x−μ)2dx=1⇒∫x∈Xeλ1−1+λ3(x−μ)2dx=eλ1−1∫x∈Xeλ3(x−μ)2dx=1=eλ1−1−πλ3=1⇒eλ1−1=−λ3π(15) \begin{aligned} \int_{x \in \mathcal{X}} e^{\lambda_1 - 1 + \lambda_3 (x - \mu)^2} dx = 1 &\Rightarrow \int_{x \in \mathcal{X}} e^{\lambda_1 - 1 + \lambda_3 (x - \mu)^2} dx \\ & = e^{\lambda_1 - 1} \int_{x \in \mathcal{X}} e^{\lambda_3 (x - \mu)^2} dx = 1 \\ &= e^{\lambda_1 - 1} \sqrt{\frac{-\pi}{\lambda_3}} = 1 \\ &\Rightarrow e^{\lambda_1 -1} = \sqrt{\frac{-\lambda_3}{\pi}} \end{aligned} \tag{15} ∫x∈X​eλ1​−1+λ3​(x−μ)2dx=1​⇒∫x∈X​eλ1​−1+λ3​(x−μ)2dx=eλ1​−1∫x∈X​eλ3​(x−μ)2dx=1=eλ1​−1λ3​−π​​=1⇒eλ1​−1=π−λ3​​​​(15)

In the above derivation, we used the following identity:

∫−∞∞e−ax2dx=πa(16) \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-ax^2} dx = \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{a}} \tag{16} ∫−∞∞​e−ax2dx=aπ​​(16)

Now, for the second constraint, we can solve it and get

∫x∈X(x−μ)2eλ1−1+λ3(x−μ)2dx=σ2⇒eλ1−1∫x∈X(x−μ)2eλ3(x−μ)2dx=σ2(17) \begin{aligned} & \int_{x \in \mathcal{X}} (x - \mu)^2 e^{\lambda_1 - 1 + \lambda_3 (x - \mu)^2} dx = \sigma^2 \\ &\Rightarrow e^{\lambda_1 - 1} \int_{x \in \mathcal{X}} (x - \mu)^2 e^{\lambda_3 (x - \mu)^2} dx = \sigma^2 \end{aligned} \tag{17} ​∫x∈X​(x−μ)2eλ1​−1+λ3​(x−μ)2dx=σ2⇒eλ1​−1∫x∈X​(x−μ)2eλ3​(x−μ)2dx=σ2​(17)

To integrate the above equation, we need to use integration by substitution. Let z=(x−μ)z =(x - \mu)z=(x−μ). Then dz=dxdz = dxdz=dx. Therefore, we have

∫x∈X(x−μ)2eλ1−1+λ3(x−μ)2dx=σ2⇒eλ1−1∫z∈Xz2eλ3z2dz=σ2⇒eλ1−1πλ33=2σ2(18) \begin{aligned} & \int_{x \in \mathcal{X}} (x - \mu)^2 e^{\lambda_1 - 1 + \lambda_3 (x - \mu)^2} dx = \sigma^2 \\ &\Rightarrow e^{\lambda_1 - 1} \int_{z \in \mathcal{X}} z^2 e^{\lambda_3 z^2} dz = \sigma^2 \\ & \Rightarrow e^{\lambda_1 - 1} \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{\lambda_3^3}} = 2 \sigma^2 \\ \end{aligned} \tag{18} ​∫x∈X​(x−μ)2eλ1​−1+λ3​(x−μ)2dx=σ2⇒eλ1​−1∫z∈X​z2eλ3​z2dz=σ2⇒eλ1​−1λ33​π​​=2σ2​(18)

The above integration uses Gamma function to solve the above integration in equation (18). The Gamma function is defined as

Γ(z)=∫0∞xz−1e−xdx,R(z)>0(19) \Gamma(z) = \int_0^{\infty} x^{z-1} e^{-x} dx, \quad \mathcal{R}(z) > 0 \tag{19} Γ(z)=∫0∞​xz−1e−xdx,R(z)>0(19)

For ∫z∈Xz2eλ3z2dz\int_{z \in \mathcal{X}} z^2 e^{\lambda_3 z^2} dz∫z∈X​z2eλ3​z2dz, and let u=λ3z2u = \lambda_3 z^2u=λ3​z2. Then du=2λ3zdzdu = 2 \lambda_3 z dzdu=2λ3​zdz and z=uλ3z = \sqrt{\frac{u}{\lambda_3}}z=λ3​u​​. Therefore, we have

∫−∞∞z2eλ3z2dz=∫0∞uλ3eudu2λ3z(u≥0)=∫0∞uλ3λ3ueudu2λ3=12λ33∫0∞ueudu=12λ33∫0∞u−1/2eudu=1λ33Γ(12)=12πλ33(20) \begin{aligned} \int_{-\infty}^\infty z^2 e^{\lambda_3 z^2} dz &= \int_{0}^\infty \frac{u}{\lambda_3} e^u \frac{du}{2\lambda_3 z} \quad \text{($u \geq 0$)} \\ & = \int_{0}^\infty \frac{u}{\lambda_3} \sqrt{\frac{\lambda_3}{u}} e^u \frac{du}{2\lambda_3 } \\ & = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{\lambda_3^3}} \int_{0}^\infty \sqrt{u} e^u du \\ & = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{\lambda_3^3}} \int_{0}^\infty u^{-1/2} e^u du \\ & = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\lambda_3^3}} \Gamma(\frac{1}{2}) \\ & = \frac{1}{2} \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{\lambda_3^3}} \end{aligned} \tag{20} ∫−∞∞​z2eλ3​z2dz​=∫0∞​λ3​u​eu2λ3​zdu​(u≥0)=∫0∞​λ3​u​uλ3​​​eu2λ3​du​=2λ33​​1​∫0∞​u​eudu=2λ33​​1​∫0∞​u−1/2eudu=λ33​​1​Γ(21​)=21​λ33​π​​​(20)

We used the special case of the Gamma function Γ(12)=π\Gamma(\frac{1}{2}) = \sqrt{\pi}Γ(21​)=π​ in the above derivation.

eλ1−1=−λ3πeλ1−1πλ33=2σ2(21) \begin{aligned} e^{\lambda_1 -1} & = \sqrt{\frac{-\lambda_3}{\pi}} \\ e^{\lambda_1 - 1} \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{\lambda_3^3}} &= 2 \sigma^2 \\ \end{aligned} \tag{21} eλ1​−1eλ1​−1λ33​π​​​=π−λ3​​​=2σ2​(21)

eλ1−1=−λ3π=2σ2λ33π⇒λ3=−12σ2(22) e^{\lambda_1 - 1} = \sqrt{\frac{-\lambda_3}{\pi}} = 2\sigma^2 \sqrt{\frac{\lambda_3^3}{\pi}} \Rightarrow \lambda_3 = - \frac{1}{2 \sigma^2} \tag{22} eλ1​−1=π−λ3​​​=2σ2πλ33​​​⇒λ3​=−2σ21​(22)

In equation (13), we have shown

p(x)=eλ1−1+λ3(x−μ)2=eλ1−1e−12σ2(x−μ)2=12πσ2e−12σ2(x−μ)2=12πσ2exp⁡(−12σ2(x−μ)2)=N(μ,σ2)(23) \begin{aligned} p(x) & = e^{ \lambda_1 - 1 + \lambda_3 (x - \mu)^2} \\ & = e^{ \lambda_1 - 1} e^{- \frac{1}{2 \sigma^2} (x - \mu)^2} \\ & = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \pi \sigma^2}} e^{- \frac{1}{2 \sigma^2} (x - \mu)^2} \\ & = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \pi \sigma^2}} \exp \left( - \frac{1}{2 \sigma^2} (x - \mu)^2 \right) \\ & = \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2) \end{aligned} \tag{23} p(x)​=eλ1​−1+λ3​(x−μ)2=eλ1​−1e−2σ21​(x−μ)2=2πσ2​1​e−2σ21​(x−μ)2=2πσ2​1​exp(−2σ21​(x−μ)2)=N(μ,σ2)​(23)

This means the probability distribution that has the maximum entropy when we have mean and variance constraints is the normal distribution. This means if a process is random, and with fixed mean and variance, only the normal distribution has the maximum entropy or gives the most information about the process.
